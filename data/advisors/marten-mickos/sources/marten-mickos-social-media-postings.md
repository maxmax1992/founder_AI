In my coaching practice, my client asked how to develop one's meticulousness / attention to detail. What do you think - did I give a useful response? Here is what I said:



1. If you were born with low attention to detail, don't expect to become the world champion of meticulousness. Be OK with who you are. But you can always improve, and it is worth it. 



Use software and AI to provide and check details. Create a habit of reviewing your own writings and calculations (perhaps 2-3 times) before sending them to a colleague. Take notes (copious notes) from meetings and conversations.



2. In addition to improving meticulousness, look for adjacent strengths of yours that can compensate. Perhaps you are a superb decision-maker. Or you move quickly to action. You might be good at listening to input from others. Build on these strengths. You will find colleagues and team members with outstanding attention to detail who want to work with you.

TEXT Lifeline / Jyri - PUBLISHED ON LINKEDIN

8 Nov 2025





The Aalto Founder Sprint cohort had the privilege of visiting Lifeline Ventures to pitch their ideas and get startup advice from Jyri Engeström and his colleague Teemu Mattila . It was an hour of intense and direct feedback, brutal at times. Jyri shared golden advice that is useful for anyone thinking of a startup. It was a terrific session.



Here is a selection of Jyri's observations and insights:



OK, so you built a prototype but you have not tried to sell it to a customer yet. Why not? - Jyri didn't spare his punches. Selling is essential, and you can start selling before you have a prototype. Sell before you build!



If your product is focused on your first narrow use case only, you are essentially claiming you can do the job of the main player in the space better than them. That's a bold claim. Or if your product is intended for a more general use case, then what's your unique differentiation? - Lifeline felt this business idea was either poorly chosen or too broad.



When you pitch to a VC, don't argue with them even if you disagree with their views. Instead, turn the table around and invite the VC to present their view of this business. You will learn something, and the room will maintain its positive and constructive vibe. There is no benefit in arguing back.



The magical thing is missing. - Jyri has learned through countless successes and failures that only startups with a magical thing can succeed wildly. The magical thing can be a realization that others haven't made, a technical innovation, a business model innovation, or something else. It has to be unique and its value must last long. 



The Oura ring, for instance, is built on the simple but powerful fact that your finger is one of the best places to measure your heartrate, and not just the rate, but the specifics of the heart beat. As a result, the ring produces detailed health signals other equipment can't even measure.





First you pitched your solution as a boon for the buyers in the marketplace. Now you say you are passionate about the other side, the selling side. Which one is it? You can't be both at once. You have to pick your unique approach.



Product-market fit (PMF) is everything. First you must understand very exactly what problem you are solving. Then you must form a testable idea of the solution. You must find some unproven or unorthodox ways of solving the problem, otherwise you will be like everyone else in that space. You must find something that is true but not known.  Until you find the basic deep truth that allows you to build a product that can reach PMF, keep looking. Just keep looking. Or keep iterating faster than anyone else. Your speed of learning will set you apart from everyone else.



Case Iceye: There are plenty of satellites with cameras. The problem is cameras don't work at night or through clouds. So how about a satellite that sees in the dark with a radar-based camera? The resolution is not as good, but you always get a picture. 





At this point, Jyri leaned forward to say something deep. He started with "yeah, I mean, you know", and we were all on our toes listening to what he would say next. It would be a description of how the best startup ecosystem functions.



Startups are a long game. Many interactions are needed, and many won't go well. Don't cut corners. Don't rush into founding your own startup. So many people just start a startup and are incredibly ill equipped to succeed. A way to learn how all of this works is to spend time working for a great startup with great founders. That's a safe recommendation in any situation. Pick a small enough company so you really get to work with the founders.



Karri Saarinen and Jori Lallo got into YCombinator with their startup Kippt. Kippt didn't do spectacularly well, but the two founders ended up at a fellow YCcombinator startup: Coinbase. They learned how startups work. From Coinbase, Karri went to AirBnB. Now, today, as founders of Linear, they have the skill and understanding of how an awesome company is built. It took many iterations and learning from the best. The results are stunningly good.

Look at the great founders of Silicon Valley. They are also investors, and they look at their business like an investor. They ask who is doing the most interesting work around, and they get close to such people. When they find promising entrepreneurs, they ask if they can put in a little bit of money and help the founders. Knowledge is shared and opportunities open up. The best founders are involved in multiple companies. More is more. They keep helping, helping, helping. The benefits accumulate and compound. Working on the right thing is more important than working hard, as Caterina Fake will say.



Thank you, Jyri and Teemu, for the awesome session with you! Aalto Founder School is grateful, and our students are now better equipped to become builders of the future.



Mårten Mickos

posted on linkedin https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7350398108012355584/



The benefits of negative feelings.



Startup CEOs live on a roller coaster. When it's great it is great and when it isn't it isn't. The ups lead to success. Indirectly, the downs lead to success too. Great CEOs make the most of every emotion.



ANGER - When we are angry, we get more energy.



SHAME - When we feel shame, we will strengthen our principles.



FEAR - When we are fearful, we may find new strategic paths.



ANXIETY - In anxiety, we can be more detail-oriented.



PAIN - In pain, we learn fast.



FATIGUE - The more exhausted we get, the more we must prioritize and focus.



ENVY - There are few benefits of envy, but some people work harder as a result.



DISAPPOINTMENT IN SOMEONE ELSE - If someone in power disappoints us, it strengthens our resolve to succeed. When it's someone we have power over, we learn to set people up for success.



DISAPPOINTMENT IN SELF - The worst feeling is to be disappointed in oneself. There is no immediate cure. Recovery starts with acknowledging one's vulnerability and weakness. Then it's time to forgive oneself and also fix the situation created. After that, and only then, can one move on and improve one's game. No one is perfect but everyone can improve. 



Every emotion has a role to play on the path to success.



There are more negative emotions and ways to turn them around. Please add your suggestions!

Soft or tough - which leadership style is best?



Pick #1 and you will be liked but you are not paying attention to results. 

Pick #2 and you will be focused on results but may not build loyalty and commitment. If you pick #3, you are neither demanding or empowering.



Contrast will do the trick. Pick #4 and combine the softest and most empowering leadership with the toughest focus on results. Your team will react well to both sides of you.

Follow your passion?



We are giving young people the wrong career advice. When we tell them to follow their passion, the result can be harmful. If they have no passion yet, the demand for it may create anxiety. If they think they have a passion, they may end up choosing the wrong career path. Simply because you enjoy a hobby does not mean it is, or will become, your professional passion. Given passion is related to identity, the young may think they should focus on developing their identity when they should focus on being useful.



Albert Einstein advised us not to try to become people of success, but rather people of value. His point was that it is not about ourselves or our identity. It is about being useful to a person, a group or society. With such a mindset, passion and identity will emerge.



There are, of course, people who find their passion early. Linus Torvalds was 21 years old when he created the Linux operating system. He is still working on it, 34 years later. If you have that much passion that early in your life, go for it!



Most young people have not developed their passion yet. What should they do for their career? They should follow great people and seek out hard problems. They should put themselves to a test of endurance and determination. 



This will lead them on a path to passion and ambition. When the time is right, they can follow their passion and break out to become the best version of themselves. 



Wishing all young people challenging and wonderful career paths!
